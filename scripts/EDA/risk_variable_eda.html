2. Visually and Quantitatively Assess for any Outliers in the Data

From the boxplots for continuous variables, it is evident that some of the NIHTB scores are likely out of the expected range. While no guidelines within the overview of the ABCD baseline neurocognition battery or mention of the extraneously high/low scores within current release notes could be located, it is likely that winsorization or other outlier removal methods will need to be applied to handle values beyond the expected range.

Following review of the NIH Toolbox (Cognition) Instrument Description (see Notes and Special Considerations), it is noted by the neurocognitive working group that For longitudinal analyses, we recommend using either uncorrected Scaled Scores or raw scores. Following review of the distributions below, a tentative decision regarding which strategy to use will be chosen.

No other anomalous or outlier values are noteworthy.

3. Appropriateness of the Mean as a Measure of Central Tendency

Though proposed clustering methods herein do not have parametric assumptions, assessment of skewness provides information about the asymmetry of variable distributions, which may help identify variables with extreme outliers or long tails. Such variables can disproportionately influence cluster formation when using euclidean distances (as in k-prototypes for continuous variables), and thus it is important to examine them. Results from the assessment of skewness values suggest that the only variable which may be problematically skewed is pgbi_p_ss_score (GBI Mania Sum Score, skewness = 3.48); though, it is expected that the variable would be positively skewed given low rates of mania in this age range.

As such, it is likely that no variable transformations are necessary, and downstream trends of this variable during clustering and subsequent frequentist analyses will just need to be monitored with this skew in mind.

Distributions of continuous variables and rates of categorical variables (including the individual ACEs comprising the sum ACE index score) are shown above. While some previously mentioned variables exhibit noticeable skew, no problematic distributions are notable here.

Per previous mention in section 2 (assessing outliers in the data), following review of the distributions above, I make the tentative choice of utilizing the uncorrected standard scores (as opposed to winsorizing the age corrected standard scores). This choice is for two key reasons:

  1. This selection aligns with the NIH Toolbox (Cognition) Instrument Description (see Notes and Special Considerations)

  2. The means, standard deviations, and full ranges of the uncorrected standard scores align more closely with the expected descriptive statistics of a measure of IQ (i.e., = 100; = 15), and contain less outliers than the age corrected standard scores of interest

This decision is tentative, however, and subject to future review/change in the event new information comes to light.
